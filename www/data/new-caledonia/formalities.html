New Caledonia - Formalities

Clearance

All yachts arriving in New Caledonia should proceed to Port Moselle, Nouméa.

All entry formalities are now only handled in Nouméa. Yachts are not permitted to stop or anchor anywhere in New Caledonian territorial waters before having visited Nouméa and completed formalities. However, if arriving after dark you are allowed to anchor in the authorised anchorages outside the entrance to the marina until the morning.

It is no longer possible to complete clearance formalities at We on Lifou Island (Loyalty Islands). It can however be "arranged" at a cost (see Lifou for more details).

On Arrival
Fly the yellow pratique flag and the French courtesy flag on entering New Caledonian waters.

Switch to VHF channel 16. At the approaches to Port Moselle, switch to channel 67 and call the Harbour Master' office.

All crew must remain on board until clearance has been completed. You can either go alongside the marina's quarantine dock (assuming there is room), or anchor out (outside the shipping channels) and dinghy into the marina to complete clearance. The latter may be quicker as you can start early and complete clearance in one day - important when crew can only go ashore once you have cleared-in.

The marina office will give you a map directing you to all the official offices. There are four governmental offices you will have to visit. They are all within walking distance and should take approx. an hour to complete. Some cruisers take their dinghy to the north end of the bay/port and have a crew member tend the dinghy while they visit the nearby clearance offices.

The Immigration office, which is near the cruise ship wharf, will need to be visited both inbound and when departing. They are only open until 1130 so visit them first. You will be asked to fill out a form and sign a register.

At Customs (open 0730-1130, 1230-1630 Monday to Thursday, and until 1400 on Friday) it is the same procedure. Ensure that the boat is placed in the Temporary Duty-free Import scheme (IFT).

Quarantine/Biosecurity are the only officials who are likely to want to come to the boat (if at anchor you will have to take them in your dinghy). They will check the boat and may confiscate food items (as outlined below). If at anchor, bring all of your forbidden foods ashore with you in plastic bags for disposal.

Quarantine / Biosecurity
BE WARNED: New Caledonia has strict quarantine regulations. Not only will the majority of your food (including frozen food) be confiscated, but all products of animal or vegetable origin, as well as items such as bikes that may be stained with soil, must be kept on board. See further details below under Customs.

Since April 2010 the quarantine clearance arrangements at Port Moselle for pets on board are uncertain as the building housing their office was demolished and the new one still under construction. See report by cruisers who arrived with a dog July 2010, as to how the situation was then. Updates from cruisers arriving here with pets would be welcome.

On Departure
On departure, Customs, Immigration, and the Port Captain have to be visited. Those planning to leave during the weekend must clear out on Friday. Port fees for 30 days (minimum length of stay) are payable when clearing out of the country with customs. There are no immigration charges when exiting.

Passports of all crew are requested by both Customs and Immigration, and Immigration will also want to see the outward tickets (or photocopies) of crew leaving by air.

Start early as the Port Captain (last person to visit) closes at 1100.

It is also now possible to clear out of any port that has a Customs office (i.e. any port where they ship minerals), but it must be arranged beforehand with the Immigration officials in Noumea.

Clearance permits 3 days after your stated departure date in which to exit New Caledonia.

It is no longer necessary to apply for a permit to visit the Loyalty Islands or Isle of Pines. On making outward clearance in Noumea, you must request such a visit a few days before leaving and the clearance will be stamped. Whilst there are reports of yachts staying up to two weeks in these Islands, normally a 24 hour stop is permitted.

In order to obtain duty free fuel, ask for a paper from Customs when you check-out stating how many litres of petrol and diesel you want (better to over-estimate). It's also possible to shop duty free with your check-out papers in town.

Immigration

All visitors (including French citizens) require a passport valid for 6 months.

The citizens of the following counties do not require a visa for visits of up to 90 days in a 6 month period:-

Countries of the EU, Andorra, Argentina, Australia, Bermuda, Bolivia, Brunei, Bermuda, Bolivia, Canada, Chile, Costa Rica, Croatia, El Salvador, Guatemala, Holy See, Honduras, Hong Kong, Japan, Macao, Malaysia, Mexico, Monaco, Nicaragua, New Zealand, Panama, Paraguay, San Marino, Singapore, South Korea, Switzerland, United States, Uruguay.

All other visitors require a visa in advance. Contact a French Consulate General at a consular section or embassy. Applications must be made in person and can take from 1 day to 3 weeks and cost 35 euro.

Immigration rules are similar to those of mainland France.

If a visa is required, note that the visa application must specify "France + New Caledonia". The visa is a 3 month non-renewable one and only one will be issued in a 6 month period. Ask for the maximum stay even if you plan to leave before that.

If arriving by plane and departing by yacht, the cabin crew will require proof of this before boarding the plane. To obtain this, the skipper must visit the Immigration Office with 2 copies of a letter giving name, flight date and number and comfirming they will be joining the yacht as crew and departing aboard the yacht. The ships papers will also be required. Both copies will be stamped and one copy of this must be faxed through to the crew before departure. It can be in English.

If leaving by plane, then both the skipper and crew (with air ticket) must go to the Immigration Office and have the crew officially removed from the crew list.

See this useful report if wanting to spend longer than 90 days or plan to leave the boat for a while.

Customs

Customs Rules

The maximum period a yacht may stay before becoming eligible for import duty is six months - or one year if no visit has been made in the previous 3 years. A six month cruising permit is granted on arrival. This relates to the boat only and crew must conform with existing visa requirements.

If the owner has to leave the country, the boat can be placed under the Custom's 'Private Warehouse' scheme for a maximum of 12 months during which time is cannot be used or moved. The limitation on the yacht's stay in new Caledonia is suspended during this time. See www.douane.gouv.ne for the necessary form. Safety and maintenance inspections can be made during storage if Customs are advised in advance.

All items belonging to the crew are permitted to be brought in duty free.

Foreign vessels may now import spare parts and equipment free of duty, but the formalities must be completed at customs in advance, either by the owner or the repair yard. Similar formalities will allow the purchase of duty-free fuel on departure.

Firearms are bonded by customs until departure.

Quarantine

New Caledonia has strict quarantine regulations. Not only will the majority of your food (including frozen food) be confiscated, but all products of animal or vegetable origin, as well as items such as bikes that may be stained with soil, must be kept on board. All such items must be declared to the Quarantine officer (SIVAP - Inspection sanitaire aux frontières).

The following food items will be confiscated: delicatessen meats, fresh/frozen meat, eggs & dairy produce, honey, fresh fruit & vegetables. Goods are taken regardless of origin (even if purchased in New Caledonia on an earlier visit) and the boat is likely to be searched for them. It is therefore best to arrive with as little fresh and frozen food as possible.

You are now allowed to keep meat purchased in Canada, Denmark, French Polynesia, Ireland (Rep. of), New Zealand, Norway, Sweden, U.S.A. or Vanuatu - providing it has the necessary New Caledonian certificate. It is best to have New Zealand meat packaged by a butcher with New Zealand Export Quality Labels.

Health

Ciguatera Warning: Cruisers who visited Chesterfield Reef, Coral Sea, August 2011, reported that there is Ciguatera in the fish there. Crew had medical problems even from fish caught in the open ocean 15 Nm from the entrance to the reef. It is therefore advisable to not eat any fish caught in or near Chesterfield Reef.

ZIKA VIRUS ALERT: (September 2016) There have been recent safety alerts from the US State Department, UK Foreign Office, and Center for Disease Control (CDC) regarding travel to parts of Central and South America, Africa, southern Asia, the Caribbean, and the South Pacific islands. There is growing concern about the rapid spread of the ZIKA Virus and the impact of the virus on pregnant women and babies. ZIKA is transmitted by mosquitos in tropical and sub-tropical climates, and there is currently no cure or vaccine. This situation is evolving rapidly, so please refer to the CDC’s dedicated website if you are intending to cruise in one of the effected areas.

Pets

For a stay of less than 2 months, on condition that the yacht remains at anchor after customs formalities are completed, animals can be confined onboard. A special form needs to be filled in, which can be obtained by email from Veterinary Services (see below).

On departure the animal's owner must inform the authorities, who will carry out an inspection.

For stays exceeding 2 months, or if the yacht comes to a berth or is on the hard, the animal must be put into quarantine, provided it has been in the Pacific for the past 6 months. Animals other than cats and dogs will not be allowed to remain onboard but must be destroyed or taken immediately out of New Caledonia.

Cats and dogs from rabies-free countries are allowed to be temporarily imported but must spend one week (5 days) in quarantine. The cost is 13,500 PCF.

New Regulations in New Caledonia for importing your dog/pet came into affect on 15th July, 2011 and all pets must complete a 10 day quarantine onboard. Contact Biosecurity who will come to your boat and check your pet out. Also contact a local vet who must then certify their visits as the first and last day of the quarantine. In between, the Regulations say the boat must stay in the bay of Noumea. Once cleared-in, New Caledonia is very pet friendly.

(Advisable to contact the a vet in advance so you make sure every day in Noumea counts towards your 10 day quarantine).
