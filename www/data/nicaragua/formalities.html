Nicaragua - Formalities

Clearance

On approach, call the port on VHF 16 to make arrangements for clearance if arriving from a foreign port.

Clearance must be made with Immigration, Customs, and Port Authorities. A number of ports have no official Port Captain and the Navy handles the ship's paperwork.

Several Nicaraguan ports require a zarpe from the previous Nicaraguan port visited, even when it is only a few miles away.

Ease of clearance and fees charged vary greatly from port to port. Even then, officials never seem to be consistent, so if possible be wary and make contact with cruisers in your intended port of entry prior to arrival to check on the current situation.

Last updated July 2016.

Immigration

Passports must be valid for at least six months.

For nationals of most countries a prior visa is not needed for visits of up to 90 days.

A tourist visa card must be purchased on entry. This new CA-4 visa applies to the following countries:- Guatemala, El Salvador, Honduras, and Nicaragua. The length of stay (normally 90 days) applies to the whole group and only one extension will usually be granted. To remain in the region longer, a visit to a country outside the CA-4 area must be made and a new visa will be issued on re-entry.

Carry a photocopy of your passport at all times.

Only citizens of the following countries must obtain visas in advance:-
Afghanistan, Albania, Armenia, Bangladesh, Bosnia and Herzegovina, Botswana, Cameroon, China, Republic of the Congo, Democratic Republic of the Congo, Eritrea, Haiti, India, Iraq, Kenya, Laos, Lebanon, Liberia, Mali, Mongolia, Nepal, Nigeria, Pakistan, Sierra Leone, Somalia, Sri Lanka, Sudan, ,Syria, Timor-Leste, Vietnam and Yemen.

Last updated July 2016.

Customs

Firearms must be declared. A declaration of money and valuables must be made on arrival.

Canned meats and dairy products are prohibited imports, also medication without accompanying prescriptions.

It has been reported that in San Juan del Sur, Customs have decided that boats are to be allowed 30 days in the country, just like a car, then will be forced to leave or pay import duties. How the import duties are calculated seems to be up to the Customs officer on charge that day. Cruisers planning on staying longer than a month in Nicaragua should determine on checking in exactly how long they are permitted to stay. There is always the option of staying 29 days, leaving for 3 and then returning.

Last updated July 2016.

Health

Malaria prophylaxis is recommended.

Dengue fever is endemic to Latin America and the Caribbean and there has been a recent significant increase in the number of reported cases.

Cases of Chikungunya virus have also been confirmed in Nicaragua.

ZIKA VIRUS ALERT: (September 2016) There have been recent safety alerts from the US State Department, UK Foreign Office, and Center for Disease Control (CDC) regarding travel to parts of Central and South America, Africa, southern Asia, the Caribbean, and the South Pacific islands. Nicaragua is an area of interest. There is growing concern about the rapid spread of the ZIKA Virus and the impact of the virus on pregnant women and babies. ZIKA is transmitted by mosquitos in tropical and sub-tropical climates, and there is currently no cure or vaccine. This situation is evolving rapidly, so please refer to the CDC’s dedicated website if you are intending to cruise in one of the effected areas.

Cases of locally transmitted Zika virus have been confirmed in the last 3 months.

Last updated July 2016.

Fees

The Transporte Acuatico now charges $50 per month for permission to navigate in Nicaraguan waters. It does not matter if you are in the water or on the hard, or only in port for one day. If you do not pay the Port Captain will not allow you a zarpe for exit.
Note however that charging of this fee is inconsistent and can be anything between $20 & $50 and in some ports (Puerta Del Sol, for example), it is not even being charged.

Tourist visa per person: US$12.50
Sailing permit (some call this Boat Import): US$25.00
National Exit Zarpe: US$10.00
International Exit Zarpe: US $25-$30

There are reports of an "anchoring fee" being charged in San Juan del Sur (40 cents per foot per day), whether you are on the hard or in the water (see San Juan del Sur for details). However latest reports from visiting cruisers is that this fee is as yet not being charged.

Note: Fees tend to vary from place to place for no apparent reason. Some officials may also attempt to solicit bribes.

Last updated December 2012.

Restrictions

To visit the Corn Islands, Bluefields and Puerto Cabezas, a permit must be obtained in Managua.

Pets

Quarantine clearance can now be done in the ports of entry other than Noumea. On arrival the port captain will inform the Quarantine service of the yacht's arrival to arrange an inspection.
