Durban - Profile

Location

This is a large and busy commercial harbour with specific areas set aside for pleasure craft.

See Tony Herrick's of Cruising Connections free e-book WELCOME TO DURBAN (A Sailor's Guide for making safe landfall in South Africa).

Clearance

See South Africa Formalities, for full details on clearing into and out of South Africa.

Durban Harbour Radio (the Signal tower that controls shipping movements into and out of the harbour) must be contacted approx. 1 mile off on VHF channel 09, to inform the authorities in advance of the yacht's arrival and to request permission to proceed through the entrance channel. Foreign yachts may be met by a Police Coast Patrol which will escort the yacht to a holding area, or alternatively Durban Harbour Radio may advise to proceed to the International Jetty or Small Craft Basin.

Arriving yachts have to contact the following departments within 24 hours of arrival: Customs, Immigration, Health, and Portnet Office. These offices must also be visited when leaving. Note all International visitors must report to Durban Immigrations, irrespective of whether they have cleared into South Africa already. Durban Marina can help arrange clearance.

If leaving for another South African port, lodge an itinerary (flight plan) with the Port Authority (time of departure, intended destination, and ETA) and clearance obtained from the yacht club or marina you are in.

NOTE: The Port Authority office is NOT open on weekends and Public Holidays, therefore clearing out should be done on the Friday morning prior to leaving.

Docking

The harbour is approached via a narrow channel, and this protects it from prevailing NE and SW winds, both of which can blow with considerable force.

Docking is available at Durban Marina or on moorings at the smaller Bluff Yacht Club, 5nm from the harbour entrance. The Royal Natal Yacht Club and the Point Yacht Club, based at the Durban Marina, no longer have berthing facilities available but there is an anchorage in front.

It is possible to anchor in the Silburn Channel, where the holding is reasonably good. Fore and aft anchors are required and care should be taken not to obstruct the channel.

In addition the club owns some moorings that are usually available for visitors, and members’ moorings that if unoccupied are also utilised to accommodate visiting boats. First come first served. The club also has a cable operated haulout facility. There is a small jetty for temporary berthing (4 hours) for watering etc. (no power or water on the jetty). Latest reports (2012) are that the Club has become rather run down, however the Club has assured noonsite that maintenance is on-going. No public transport from Club to Durban, however taxis available and members are very friendly offering lifts. Limited local amenities.

In the heart of the city, close to the Golden Mile. This marina has over 350 pontoon berths as well as 200 swinging moorings, however space for visitors is limited. Limited shore power, drinking water available on pontoons. Reported to be clean with helpful staff. Marina can help with clearance formalities. Internet services are available at the two Yacht clubs, as are their restaurants and bars. Useful arrival information on the marina website.
Incoming boats should clear into the Durban Marina office on arrival where the clearing in procedure for Customs and Immigrations will be explained, as well as the clearing out procedure. Note that from November to February each year is the busy season. Boats are requested to anchor and report into the Marina Office for possible berth allocation.

Yacht Clubs

The Point Yacht Club and Royal Natal Yacht Club have internet facilities, as well as good bars and restaurants, which visiting yachtsmen are welcome to use when berthed at the Durban Marina. Yachtsmen have a week’s free membership with either club to use their facilities.
