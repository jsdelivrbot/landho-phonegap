Solomon Islands - Formalities

Clearance

Clearing - In

Clear in at an official Port of Entry first. On arrival, immediately report to the authorities and request clearance. This is usually staight forward, but obtaining a clearance pack before arrival will speed up the process. See www.commerce.gov.sb to download the forms.

Stopping and going ashore at any island before clearing in is strictly prohibited and boats that do this will pay a heavy fine. This is particularly true of the eastern islands, such as Tikopia or Vanikolo, where yachts coming from Vanuatu have stopped without first clearing into the Solomons at Ndende (Nendo) Island.

Whilst Noonsite has received reports from cruisers who successfully anchored in several places in the eastern and western Solomons before officially checking in (not encountering enforcement boats), it is recommended to not take the risk of being fined.

Note:

  • Lata (Nendo/Ndende Island, Santa Cruz) only has Customs and Quarantine. You will need to attend Immigration in Honiara.

 

Clearing - Out

Clearance out of the Solomons can be done from any Port of Entry.

Note that clearance fees and, for some places, anchorage fees are now being collected. See "Fees" below for the latest details.

Immigration

Passports are required by all visitors and must be valid for 6 months beyond your stay.

Visas are not required for up to a three month stay for U.S., British Commonwealth and most European citizens, but there is an extensive list of nationals from various countries who do require prior approval before visiting the islands.

See Updated list

Nationals of countries who do require a visa must apply to the Director of Immigration (see details below).

A visitors permit will be issued for a period not exceeding three months in any period of twelve months. Extensions can be obtained for up to another 3 months if there are special circumstances, for which there is a fee of SBD$30 per month.

Arriving by Plane

Crew who plan to arrive in the Solomon Islands by plane and to depart on a yacht should apply for a visitor’s permit to the Director of Immigration before their arrival. The application should state the crew’s arrival date, vessel name and registration details, vessel’s arrival date, approximate length of time the crew member will spend in the Solomon Islands, and it should request entry on a one-way (arrival only) airline ticket. The Director will issue a permit to be presented at airline check-in. If the crew member does not have this permit, they may be denied boarding.

In Dec 2010, new visa requirements were being considered which will require ALL visitors to obtain a visa in advance of arrival. It is planned that such visas will be obtainable via the internet but as yet, there is no timetable for implementation.

Customs

Firearms must be declared, otherwise they may be forfeited. Firearms and alcohol will be sealed on board.

Fruit and vegetables are permitted on board, but you will be asked not to bring them ashore.

Exports of genuine artifacts, not replicas, require permission.

The Solomon Islands' government prohibits the export of military artifacts from World War II, as well as the import of religious materials and antiquities.

A yacht may stay in the country for a total of six months. An initial three months will be granted on rival and this can be extended for another three months.

Health

Malaria is endemic in the Solomons and malarial prophylaxis is absolutely essential. Many cruising sailors have caught malaria in the Solomons recently, so the usual precautions should also be taken, such as having mosquito screens on all portholes and hatches, anchoring further offshore and possibly upwind from any village, trying to avoid going ashore in the late afternoon or early evening when mosquitoes are most active.

Tap water is not potable and should be treated everywhere including Honiara and Gizo.

The hospitals often run out of basic supplies such as antibiotics and blood supplies.

Danger - Crocodiles

Salt water crocodiles are native to parts of the Solomon Islands, and there have been sightings on beaches. On arrival in any new place, ensure you ask locals whether there are any crocodiles. Local advice should be sought before entering unfamiliar waters, including lakes.

Fees

Attempt to arrive during normal working hours. In most places it is possible to wait at anchor until the offices open, but no one should go ashore before clearance is completed.

There is a clearance fee of US$18 per metre.

There are also immigration charges of SI$300 (US$45) and a SI$150 (US$25) quarantine fee.

Immigration: To extend a visitor's permit for 6 weeks costs SI$200 per person.

In some places there is now a daily charge for anchoring.
The Florida islands group has initiated a charge of SI$300 per day.
It has been reported that at the far eastern island of Tikopia in the reef islands, a yacht was charged SI$500.

Try and establish to whom you are paying the fee as it may be asked for twice!

Last updated April 2015.

Restrictions

The authorities in Honiara will advise where a yacht can or cannot go. There are usually no problems in calling at the main centres or at islands used to cruising yachts.

Once anchored, go ashore to see the chief or village head and ask permission to anchor. If you wish to swim or fish you should also ask permission. This is usually never a problem and you will often be warmly welcomed.

In most places locals will happily trade their coconuts and other fresh produce with you.

Fishing and collecting shells are not normally allowed unless one has been given permission, nor are coconuts and produce to be taken.

Local Customs

The wearing of shorts or other scanty clothing by women is not allowed.

Dress codes, particularly for women, are modest and you will gain respect by dressing appropriately. In certain areas there are ‘taboo’ sites only visited by men.

Standards of dress and behaviour in Solomon Islands is conservative, and you should take care not to offend. Public displays of affection and swearing in public may cause offence.

The Islanders have many traditional "kastoms" which vary from province to province. Always ask permission before taking pictures of people or places.

Marovo Lagoon: Most islanders are Seventh Day Adventists so their Sabbath is from sunset on Friday to sunset on Saturday, and visitors are not supposed to go ashore here during the Sabbath.

Pets

Animals are not to be landed. If for any reason they are landed, they will be subject to quarantine.

Dogs and cats can only be imported from Australia, New Zealand and the United Kingdom.
