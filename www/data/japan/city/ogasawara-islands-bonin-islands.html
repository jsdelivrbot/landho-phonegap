Ogasawara Islands (Bonin Islands) - Profile

Location

This archipelago of over 30 subtropical and tropical islands lie in the Pacific Ocean some 540 miles south of Honshu.

If you are voyaging to Japan from the East it is a natural Port of Entry to Japan.

Because the Ogasawara Islands have never been connected to a continent, many of their animals and plants have undergone unique evolutionary processes. For this reason the islands are often referred to as the 'Galapagos of the Orient'and in 2011 gained World Heritage Natural Site listing.

The islands have an interesting history being claimed by various countries, with the first permanent settlement established by ex-whalers from the USA in the 1830s. The islands changed sovereignty a couple of times through various colonial period machinations and Pacific activities of the second world war, with them being returned to Japan in 1968.

Ogasawara is the administrative center of the island group, with most major government agencies centered here.

Clearance

Ogasawara has the intriguing status of being both a closed port and a port of entry. Upon entry the Coast Guard will direct you to a slot on the concrete pier (fender well) and then you will be visited by all the normal government representatives.

At least 24hrs notice of arrival is required as per regular first entry to Japan, and having a list prepared for your following ports within Japan with marina names etc. will smooth the paperwork no end.

Docking

The port in Ogasawara (Futami Ko) is home to both the fishing and tourist boat industries and is all concrete docks.  Some charts show a sector light for port entrance, this was not functioning in July 2014.  The local Coast Guard office in Ogasawara does not stock Japanese charts for sale or duplication, so ensure you have charts with you for your landfall in the main island chain.

It appears that a resident local high pressure system offers some insulation from typhoons that regularly visit the main island chain, however the port is somewhat open to the west, so typhoon activity in that area may make the port uncomfortable and at times untenable.

The islands are serviced by a twice weekly ferry from Tokyo, their only commercial passenger link.

Toilet facilities abound on the island, all being clean, lit and
safe, with two public outdoor showers at the beaches, the
closest being 10min walk from the main harbour, making a day ending swim and shower a nice precursor to dinner at one of the many great local restaurants and bars on the way back to the boat.

Fees: The cruiser who passed on this port information was not charged a docking fee for his stay, however it was unclear if this was policy to offer short term visitors a free stay, or if the language barrier made it a difficult subject to approach.

A note re. Rubbish Disposal

Most prefectures in Japan separate their rubbish prior to disposal. To save yourself a very messy job on arrival, try to organise your rubbish into the following categories en route;
- plastic PET drink bottlers;
- tin cans;
- aluminum cans;
- general waste.

Whilst the exact categories may vary slightly from prefecture to prefecture, sorting as you go into these four groups should make things easier on arrival.
