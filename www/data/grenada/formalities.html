Grenada - Formalities

Clearance

The SailClear advance web notification service for clearance is now operational here. Users previously registered with the old eSeaClear system will find all their details have been carried over to the new system.

For a full explanation of this service and other web clearance services in the Caribbean see report: Eastern Caribbean Cruising: Clearance Procedures Simplified

Proceed to a Port of Entry, anchor, and fly the Q flag clearly until the formalities have been completed. Arrival must be notified within 2 hours.

Documents to be prepared are ships's documentation papers. A simplified new check-in/out clearance form can be downloaded off the internet prior to arrival (see below).

Grenada has a combined Immigration/Customs/Ports Authority Form to ease the clearance procedures. See here for the Clearance form

Documents required are 4 copies of the crew list, visas, immigration cards and passports for all the crew, Ships, stores and health declaration, and port clearance from last port of call.

Customs and immigration offices at marine bases are generally open, Mon to Fri from 08:00-11:45 and 13:00-16:00 Monday to Thursday; Friday 08:00-11:45 and 13:00-17:00; Weekends from 09:00-13:00. Some variation in the hours exists at of the ports of entry. Overtime is charged outside office hours.

When departing, you may clear out during normal working hours and leave within 24 hours. It is advisable to attend the office by 15:00 in order to complete formalities before the end of the working day.

If departing for Trinidad, it is now possible to file a passage plan with the coastguard. See report here for further details.

Immigration

Passport must be valid for 6 months beyond your stay.

A visa is not required from citizens of the US, Canada, UK, British Commonwealth, Caribbean countries, most European countries, South Korea, and Japan.

All others require visas, although a visa-free stay may be granted on arrival if continuing on to another destination within 14 days.

See Grenada Consulate for a complete list. Download a visa application form here

In recognition of Grenada’s popularity as a long term ‘base’ for yachts, Grenada Immigration has changed its policy on extension of visas for yachting visitors.

A 3 month stay is now generally granted on arrival if requested, but this can subsequently be extended up to 12 months. Note that the first 3 months are free, however after that time the visa needs to be extended monthly with a monthly charge (see Fees below). 

Once the 12 months period has been reached, there is now no requirement to leave and re-enter.  If Immigration are satisfied that the visitor has the means to leave should the need arise, and has not violated the terms of their visitor visa, they have the discretion to extend the visa for up to another 12 months if requested.  At this point another 12 month stay can be requested.

To obtain an extension, each crew member must present their passport to either the Immigration Officer at the main office, Botanical Gardens, St. Georges, or at the office in Hillsborough, Carriacou.

If you have crew leaving the boat and flying out of Grenada, visit the Immigration Officer prior to the day of departure and request the form for each person that is usually completed on an incoming flight. The stub of that form can be presented to Immigration at the airport at departure time. That form will be presented to the Immigration officials at the airport during departure processing. Grenada has a EC$50 per person (EC$25 if under age 12) departure fee at the airport. If you are flying in to return to your vessel be sure to have a copy of your vessel documentation and clearance papers to show at the airport, or if you are joining a vessel have a letter from the Captain of the vessel stating that you will be joining the vessel.

There is an embarkation tax of EC$20.  This is paid on departure by yacht, and is levied on each person aboard who is not ‘bone fida’ crew (i.e. passengers and those not involved in the operation of the yacht).  Typically this will not be levied on a cruising ‘couple’ running their yacht, but may be on any additional people on board at the discretion of the Immigration Officer.

Customs

Customs issue a monthly cruising permit (see fees below).

Firearms must be declared to Customs and will be sealed on board in a proper locker or kept ashore in custody until departure. A receipt will be issued by the police.

Medicines such as morphine in the medical stores must be declared.

Importing Yacht Spares

Foreign flagged yachts may import parts and equipment free of duty (Yacht In Transit). The parts are usually detained at Customs, or the post office, or the freight company warehouse and, to retrieve them, one needs to obtain a stamped form (C14) from the Customs office where one has cleared in.

If arriving by air, this will mean leaving your parts at the airport while you complete the paperwork and then returning to collect (unless you use an agent).

An invoice or bill showing the value of the goods (even if there is no charge to you i.e. warranty or replacement goods, including carriage) must be presented and Customs will charge a fee (about 2.5%, although it may be more) on the declared value.

For large or expensive items (over $3000 EC) a Customs agent is required to process the paperwork. Some of the boatyards will provide assistance and their agent to facilitate the clearance of your "Yacht in Transit" item.

Island Water World will also provide assistance clearing in "Yacht In Transit" items providing it is not an item that is normally sold within their stores.

Most chandleries and boat service companies will provide products and services at "duty free" prices if you present your ship's documentation and the Grenada Customs/Immigration papers you were issued upon arrival.

It can take between 3 to 14 days for the goods to actually be released.

Note: Cruisers report being treated unfairly when bringing spares in by plane to Grenada.

Health

ZIKA VIRUS ALERT: (September 2016) There have been recent safety alerts from the US State Department, UK Foreign Office, and Center for Disease Control (CDC) regarding travel to parts of Central and South America, Africa, southern Asia, the Caribbean, and the South Pacific islands. Grenada is an area of interest with multiple reported cases and active virus transmission. There is growing concern about the rapid spread of the ZIKA Virus and the impact of the virus on pregnant women and babies. ZIKA is transmitted by mosquitos in tropical and sub-tropical climates, and there is currently no cure or vaccine. This situation is evolving rapidly, so please refer to the CDC’s dedicated website if you are intending to cruise in one of the effected areas.

Documents

A cruising permit, valid for one year maximum, must be obtained on arrival - but a new one must be obtained every time the boat arrives again from a foreign country, even if that is within the validity of the last cruising permit issued.

Boats up to 40 ft pay EC$50, Boats up to 60 ft pay 75 EC, Boats up to 80 ft pay 100 EC.

Fees

Grenada Entry Charges
The charges (EC$) are as follows -

Cruising Permit Fee (per yacht):
Less than 40 ft EC$50
Over 40 ft and less than 60 ft EC$75
Over 60 ft and less than 80 ft EC$100
Over 80 ft EC$150
These charges are now PER MONTH, not per cruise as previously.

Prickly Bay Customs now accept credit cards. Immigration however does not, so some cash still needed here.

Cruise Levy (per person) EC$8.10

Overtime fees are charged outside office hours and on Saturdays, Sundays and public holidays.

A small charge is made for the crew list form if one does not provide one's own.

Visa extension after the first initial 3 months free visa: EC$25 per month.

Crew arriving by plane and leaving by boat, are required to pay an Embarkation Tax of $EC20 per person, to the Immigration office at the time of departure. On departure of the yacht, this fee is also levied on each person aboard who is not ‘bone fida’ crew - passengers and those not involved in the operation of the yacht. It is applied at the discretion of the Immigration Officer.

Arriving by boat and departing by airplane costs EC$50 per person (EC$25 if under 12 y.o.) payable at the airport.

Note an official receipt should be issued for all monies paid.

Restrictions

Public nudity in Grenada is against the law. Local residents are concerned about nudity on yachts anchored close to the shore and cruisers are asked to respect the rules of Grenada society and keep their clothes on.

Yachts may not anchor in front of the hotels at the south end of Grand Anse Beach.

Lobster and spear fishing is prohibited without a licence, which is issued only to Grenadians. Lobster should only be bought in season (31 October to 30 April).

It is prohibited to pump bilge or waste into the water, and marine toilets may not be used within 200 metres of the beaches. There are toilets ashore at both marinas and the yacht clubs.

Yachts are urged to observe the 1.5 km exclusion zone around the undersea volcano Kick 'Em Jenny, located west of Isle de Ronde off Grenada's northwest coast (12.18°N 61.38°W).

The volcano continues to pose a threat to vessels. During an eruption an exclusion zone of 5 km is enforced, ie all vessels should stay at least 10km west of Isle de Ronde. More details are available at the Seismic Unit's webpage dedicated to cruising information. http://www.uwiseismic.com/SRU_Site01/KeJ/kejyachting.html

Pets

WARNING:
Poision is regularly used where sheep and goats are roaming free. Read this report so you can take due care of your pets when visiting Grenada.

Procedure for Pets

A Health Certificates/documents and innoculation records must be produced, if requested, for animals on transient yachts. Normally shipboard animals are routinely allowed entry without any hassles.

The procedure for the formal importation of animals in general (dogs and cats in particular) is as follows:-

A Veterinary officer or an officer of the Pest Management Unit of the Ministry of Agriculture must inspect the animals prior to entry.

Any person intending to import or take these animals from their vessels, whether permanent or temporary, must make an application sufficiently in advance to any of the above agencies.

Such an application must be accompanied by a copy of a certificate from the last place of residence certifying that the animal has been vaccinated against diseases and is free from illnesses and infections.
